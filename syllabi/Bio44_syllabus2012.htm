Course goals: Together with Bio 43L, this course is a gateway to the science of biology. We cover a broad swath of biology with the twin goals of exposing you to as many different research questions and disciplines of biology as possible and giving you a solid foundation in biological concepts for your upper level electives. Introductory Biology is as much about inspiring you with the diversity of biological research, as it is about any particular topic. You will learn a little about a lot of different topics, with the idea that you can use your upper division electives to pursue the topics you find most interesting. We will cover four main, broad topics this semester: 1) Evolution and phylogenetics, 2) Diversity and the history of life, 3) Plant biology, and 3) Ecology and population biology.

 

By the end of the semester you should:

·  understand the basic principles of evolution and how they unify all of biology.

·  develop a basic knowledge of the history and diversity of life on earth.

·  more fully understand how biotic and abiotic factors affect the distribution, abundance and behavior of organisms.

·  further develop skills in learning science as a process of asking and addressing questions rather than a collection of facts.

Course Elements

 

1) Lectures: Attending lectures is strongly encouraged, as 90% of the exam material will be covered in lectures (remaining 10% will be based on the readings & case studies). Please arrive on time and avoid disturbing other students by talking, texting, or snoring during lecture. Cell phones must be silent. I will not be taking attendance in lectures, but your participation in class activities will count towards your final grade.

 

2) Readings: The textbook covers a broad range of biological research, and we will only highlight a subset of these concepts in depth during lectures. You should treat the textbook as resource for inspiring your interest in biology. Reading the assigned chapters before each lecture is strongly encouraged, as you will get the most out of lectures if you are already familiar with the concepts being discussed. I will provide lecture outlines before each class, to help you focus your reading.

 

3) Case Studies: Three time throughout the semester, we will discuss 1-2 journal articles in small groups and you will have to submit written answers to a series of questions after the discussion. The case studies are designed to help you think in more depth about the topics we cover in lecture.

 

4) Quizzes. There will be six short quizzes spread throughout the semester. Quiz days will be announced 1 or 2 lectures in advance. Each quiz is worth 5 points, and you can drop the lowest of your 6 quiz grades.

 

5) Exams. There will be two midterm exams and a final. The final exam will be 50% on the last third of the course and 50% cumulative. The dates of all exams are given in the lecture schedule, so check them with your semester calendar. Note that the midterm exams are in the evenings, not during normal class hours! If you have a conflict with one of the evening exams, you must notify me before Friday, January 27th. If you need to miss an exam because of a personal or family emergency, please contact your Dean of Students/Office of Student Affairs.

 

6) Class Participation. Lectures are not expected to be a passive experience. I encourage questions and discussion during lecture, to this end, you can receive up to 20 points for class participation.

 

7) Labs: The lab portion of the class is designed to help reinforce some of the biological concepts we discuss in class and also give you experience in designing and analyzing experiments. The labs are coordinated by Dr. Susan Schenk.

 

Grading: Your grade is based on two midterm exams, class participation, case studies, lab work, and a final exam. Late assignments will be docked 5% for each class day late. Note that you must receive 50% of lecture points and 50% of the lab points to pass the class
